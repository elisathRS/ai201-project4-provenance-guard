# Project Planning - Provenance Guard 

## 1. Detection Signals and Combination Logic

The multi-signal pipeline utilizes two distinct analytical engines to evaluate text.

### Signal 1: Semantic Analyzer (LLM-Based)
* **What it measures:** Narrative flow, logical coherence, semantic predictability, and overall stylistic "vibe." It acts as a qualitative evaluator.
* **Output:** A float value between `0.0` (Highly likely Human) and `1.0` (Highly likely AI).

### Signal 2: Stylometric Heuristics (Python Statistical Engine)
* **What it measures:** The mathematical footprint of the text, specifically sentence length variance (burstiness) and type-token ratio (vocabulary diversity).
* **Output:** A float value between `0.0` (Highly variable/Human) and `1.0` (Highly uniform/AI).

### Combination Logic (The Semantic Veto)
We do not use a strict 50/50 average, as this allows broken heuristics to pollute valid semantic readings. The Scoring Engine dynamically weighs the signals:
* **Baseline:** 60% Semantic, 40% Stylometric.
* **The Veto:** If the Semantic LLM score strongly indicates human authorship (e.g., `< 0.25`) but the Stylometric score indicates AI (e.g., `> 0.75`), the engine assumes the text is human-authored but heavily edited (the "Grammarly Trap"). A penalty is applied to the Stylometric score, heavily weighting the final output toward the Semantic reading.

## 2. Uncertainty Representation

A confidence score of `0.60` does not mean the system is merely "guessing." It explicitly represents that the system detects conflicting signals—for example, the text features highly uniform, rigid sentence structures (triggering the stylometric AI flag) but contains idiosyncratic metaphors and emotional leaps (triggering the semantic human flag). It mathematically represents *mixed* or *heavily edited* text.

To prevent false positives and protect creators, we utilize wide safety bands for our thresholds:
* **0.00 – 0.20:** High-confidence Human
* **0.21 – 0.84:** Uncertain / Mixed Origin (This wide band is our safety net)
* **0.85 – 1.00:** High-confidence AI

---

## 3. Transparency Label Design

The numerical confidence score is never shown to the end reader. Instead, it maps strictly to one of three plain-language UI labels:

### High-Confidence AI (Score 0.85 – 1.00)
> **Automated Content:** Our analysis indicates with high confidence that this text was generated by AI.

### Uncertain Result (Score 0.21 – 0.84)
> **Uncertain Origin:** This text exhibits mixed signals and may contain a blend of human writing and automated editing tools.

### High-Confidence Human (Score 0.00 – 0.20)
> **Human Authored:** Our analysis indicates this text was written by a human without significant AI assistance.

## 4. Appeals Workflow

When a creator feels their work has been misclassified, the system provides a structured contestation path.

* **Who can appeal:** The authenticated author of the submitted content.
* **Information provided:** The system captures the specific `submission_id` and a free-text reasoning field where the creator explains their process (e.g., "I used Grammarly to check passive voice," or "Attached is my outline").
* **System Action:** Upon receiving the `POST /appeal` payload, the system updates the attribution status in the database from `active` to `under_review`. It then appends the creator's written reasoning directly to the existing JSON record in the Audit Log.
* **The Human Reviewer View:** When a moderator opens the admin dashboard (built with React/TypeScript), they do not just see the appeal in isolation. They see a unified view containing:
    * The original text snippet.
    * The individual signal scores (LLM vs. Math) highlighting where the system got confused.
    * The final combined score and assigned label.
    * The user's written appeal.

## 5. Anticipated Edge Cases

The system is designed with known blind spots that will require ongoing monitoring.

### Edge Case 1: Heavily Edited Academic Text (The Grammarly Trap)
A human writes a formal research paper or legal brief and uses strict grammar checkers before submission. The stylometric engine will detect near-zero burstiness (uniform sentence lengths) and a standardized vocabulary. Even with the Semantic Veto, highly technical jargon paired with perfect grammar may cause both signals to misclassify the human text as high-confidence AI.

### Edge Case 2: Stylized Short Poetry
A human submits a four-line poem with a rigid, repetitive meter (like a Haiku) and simple vocabulary. Because the text is incredibly short, the stylometric engine lacks the data points to calculate a statistically significant variance. Furthermore, the semantic LLM may interpret the rigid structural constraints of the poem as artificial generation, leading to an inaccurate AI label. *(Mitigation: Submissions under 100 words should automatically bypass stylometric checks).*
